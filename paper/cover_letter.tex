\opening{Dear Editor,}

I am pleased to submit the manuscript entitled \textbf{``Non-Directional Pre-Disclosure Drift Under Fair Disclosure: Evidence from Korean Earnings Announcements''} for consideration in the \textit{Pacific-Basin Finance Journal}.

This study examines the long-term effectiveness of South Korea's Fair Disclosure regulation using 2,221 earnings disclosure filings from 64 KOSPI-listed companies across 12 chaebol groups (2020--2026). Our key finding is counterintuitive: while statistically significant pre-disclosure abnormal returns exist under parametric tests, this drift does not predict the direction of actual earnings surprises (49\% accuracy, no better than chance). In contrast, event-day returns respond sharply and correctly to earnings news ($p$<0.001). We conclude that Korea's Fair Disclosure regime effectively prevents directional information leakage.

The manuscript makes several contributions well-suited to PBFJ:
\begin{itemize}
    \item It provides the first long-term assessment of Korea's Fair Disclosure regime, demonstrating that the regulation continues to function effectively more than two decades after implementation.
    \item It introduces a critical methodological distinction between the \textit{existence} of pre-disclosure drift and its \textit{directional informativeness}, showing that the former does not imply the latter.
    \item It documents that 96.5\% of DART fair disclosure filings occur after market close---a previously overlooked institutional feature with important implications for event study design.
    \item The findings have direct policy relevance for Pacific-Basin securities regulators evaluating fair disclosure frameworks.
\end{itemize}

The manuscript has not been previously published and is not under consideration elsewhere. All data sources are publicly available. Analysis code is at \url{https://github.com/yjkim0123/pre-disclosure-leakage}.

\closing{Sincerely,}
