\begin{abstract}
Does South Korea's Fair Disclosure regulation effectively ensure that earnings information reaches all market participants simultaneously? Using 2,221 earnings disclosure filings from 64 KOSPI-listed companies across 12 chaebol groups (2020--2026), we find a seemingly paradoxical pattern: statistically significant pre-disclosure abnormal returns coexist with strong evidence that Fair Disclosure \textit{works}. While the market model yields a pre-disclosure CAR[-5,$-$1] of +0.52\% ($t$=4.65, $p$<0.001), this drift does not predict the direction of actual earnings surprises (49.0\% accuracy, $p$=0.649; Spearman $\rho$=$-$0.006, $p$=0.876). Non-parametric tests confirm that the drift is driven by outliers rather than systematic directional movement (sign test $p$=0.585; Wilcoxon $p$=0.142). In contrast, event-day returns respond sharply and correctly to earnings surprises: positive surprises generate CAR[0,+1] of +0.81\%, negative surprises $-$0.47\% ($p$<0.001). We further document that 96.5\% of filings occur after market close, making the event-day return effectively pre-disclosure and the first true market reaction occurring on Day +1. Our findings suggest that Korea's Fair Disclosure regime successfully prevents directional information leakage, even though non-directional price noise and abnormal volume emerge in the pre-disclosure period.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Pre-announcement drift in stock prices is a well-documented phenomenon across global equity markets \citep{kaniel2012individual}. When stock prices move before official earnings announcements, a natural concern arises: is material information leaking to select market participants in violation of fair disclosure regulations? This concern motivates securities regulators worldwide to mandate simultaneous public dissemination of material information.

South Korea implemented its Fair Disclosure regulation in November 2002, requiring listed companies to disclose material information simultaneously to all market participants through the DART (Data Analysis, Retrieval and Transfer) electronic filing system. While early studies found evidence of improved disclosure environments following the regulation's adoption \citep{yun2012fair, park2020disclosure}, whether the regulation continues to be effective over two decades later remains an open question.

This study examines the long-term effectiveness of Korea's Fair Disclosure regulation by analyzing 2,221 earnings disclosure filings from 64 major KOSPI-listed companies between 2020 and 2026. Our approach differs from prior work in a critical respect: rather than simply documenting the existence of pre-disclosure price movement, we test whether such movement is \textit{directionally informative}---that is, whether it predicts the sign of the actual earnings surprise.

Our findings reveal a seemingly paradoxical pattern. On the one hand, we observe statistically significant pre-disclosure abnormal returns under standard parametric tests (CAR[-5,$-$1] = +0.52\%, $p$<0.001 under the market model). On the other hand, this drift bears no systematic relationship to the direction of actual earnings surprises. Pre-disclosure returns predict surprise direction with only 49.0\% accuracy---no better than a coin flip ($p$=0.649). Non-parametric tests (sign test, Wilcoxon signed-rank) fail to confirm the parametric result, indicating that the observed mean drift is driven by outliers rather than a pervasive pattern.

In contrast, the market responds sharply and correctly to the actual earnings news on the disclosure date. Positive earnings surprises generate a CAR[0,+1] of +0.81\%, while negative surprises generate $-$0.47\%, a highly significant difference ($p$<0.001). This finding is consistent with effective Fair Disclosure: material information is incorporated into prices only when it becomes publicly available.

We make four contributions. First, we provide the first long-term assessment of Korea's Fair Disclosure regime using recent data spanning the COVID-19 and post-pandemic periods. Second, we demonstrate the critical distinction between the \textit{existence} of pre-disclosure drift (which is ubiquitous) and its \textit{directional informativeness} (which would indicate information leakage). Third, we document a previously overlooked institutional feature---96.5\% of DART fair disclosure filings are submitted after market close---that has important implications for event study interpretation. Fourth, we provide cross-sectional and temporal analyses that reveal significant heterogeneity across chaebol groups, fiscal quarters, and years.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:literature}

\subsection{Fair Disclosure Regulation}

Fair disclosure regulation addresses selective disclosure, whereby corporate insiders share material information with favored market participants before public release \citep{heflin2003regulation}. In the U.S., Regulation Fair Disclosure (Reg FD), implemented in 2000, produced mixed evidence: \citet{ahmed2003impact} find reduced information asymmetry, while \citet{heflin2003regulation} document reduced information quality.

In Korea, \citet{yun2012fair} examined the effect of fair disclosure regulation on the timeliness and informativeness of earnings announcements, while \citet{park2020disclosure} investigated how disclosure frequency under the regulation affects the information environment and cost of capital. More recently, \citet{shin2023korea} studied the effects of Korea's compulsory preliminary earnings announcements on stock prices. However, these studies do not examine whether pre-disclosure price movements are directionally informative with respect to the actual earnings news---the central question of our study.

\subsection{Pre-Announcement Drift and Its Interpretation}

Pre-announcement drift in stock prices is well-documented but its interpretation is debated. \citet{meulbroek1992comparison} provides evidence of illegal insider trading preceding announcements. \citet{bernile2016can} show that informed trading occurs even ahead of macro-news announcements where information is tightly controlled. However, \citet{kaniel2012individual} demonstrate that individual investor trading patterns around earnings announcements can generate drift without requiring illegal information leakage.

Critically, the mere existence of pre-announcement drift does not establish information leakage. Predictable earnings patterns allow sophisticated investors to position ahead of announcements through legal means \citep{atiase1985predisclosure}. \citet{chae2005trading} shows that abnormal volume before announcements reflects information asymmetry but may arise from varying levels of investor sophistication rather than illegal tip-offs. A related literature on post-earnings-announcement drift (PEAD) demonstrates that markets may take days or weeks to fully incorporate earnings information \citep{bernard1989post, bernard1990evidence}, further complicating the interpretation of short-window event studies.

\subsection{Event Study Methodology}

The standard event study framework \citep{brown1985using, mackinlay1997event} relies on parametric tests of mean abnormal returns. However, \citet{kolari2010event} demonstrate that cross-sectional correlation of abnormal returns can substantially inflate test statistics, particularly when events cluster in calendar time. \citet{patell1976corporate} introduced standardized abnormal returns to improve the power of event study tests. We incorporate both non-parametric alternatives and standardized returns to address these concerns.

\subsection{Filing Time and Market Microstructure}

The timing of information release within the trading day affects how markets process information. \citet{lee1993market} shows that market microstructure features influence price discovery, while \citet{berkman2009sell} document differences in trading patterns around earnings announcements related to differences of opinion. \citet{bamber1997trading} demonstrate that trading volume around announcements reflects disagreement among investors. Our study is among the first to systematically classify DART filing times and demonstrate their implications for event study interpretation in the Korean market.

\subsection{Korean Market Context}

The Korean stock market features concentrated chaebol ownership, a mandatory electronic disclosure system (DART), significant retail investor participation, and relatively strict insider trading regulations \citep{lee2019regulation}. \citet{kim2002market} show that market liquidity and volume patterns around earnings announcements are informative about the information environment. These institutional features, combined with the comprehensive DART database, make Korea an ideal setting for testing fair disclosure effectiveness.

\section{Data and Methodology}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Data Sources}

We collect all \begin{CJK}{UTF8}{mj}영업실적 공정공시\end{CJK} (Fair Disclosure of Operating Results) filings from DART between January 2020 and March 2026 using the OpenDartReader API. We focus on original filings, excluding corrections (\begin{CJK}{UTF8}{mj}기재정정\end{CJK}). Our sample comprises 2,221 disclosures from 64 companies across 12 chaebol groups: Samsung (12), Hyundai Motor (8), LG (8), SK (7), HD Hyundai (5), Hanwha (5), POSCO (4), Lotte (4), Doosan (4), CJ (3), KT (2), and GS (2). Daily stock price and volume data are obtained from FinanceDataReader.

\subsection{Filing Time Classification}

We classify filing times using the DART receipt number prefix. In our sample, 96.5\% of earnings disclosures carry the ``800'' prefix, indicating filing after the 15:30 KST market close (Table~\ref{tab:filing_time}). This has fundamental implications: for these filings, the Day~0 return is entirely pre-disclosure, and the first trading day where the market can react is Day~+1.

\subsection{Market Model}

We employ the market model with a [-250,$-$30] estimation window \citep{mackinlay1997event}:
\begin{equation}
R_{i,t} = \alpha_i + \beta_i R_{m,t} + \epsilon_{i,t}
\end{equation}
where $R_{m,t}$ is the KOSPI index return. We require a minimum of 60 trading days in the estimation window, yielding 2,208 events with valid estimates. The average estimated beta is 0.978. For robustness, we also report market-adjusted returns ($\alpha=0$, $\beta=1$).

\subsection{Earnings Surprise Measure}

We extract year-over-year (YoY) operating profit changes from the disclosure texts. Events where YoY operating profit increases are classified as positive surprises; decreases as negative surprises. Of the 2,208 events with valid market model estimates, 584 have parseable earnings surprise data: 358 positive and 226 negative surprises.

\subsection{Abnormal Volume}

Abnormal volume is the ratio of event-day volume to the average daily volume during [-25,$-$6]:
\begin{equation}
AV_{i,t} = V_{i,t} \bigg/ \frac{1}{20}\sum_{\tau=-25}^{-6} V_{i,\tau}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Statistical Tests}

We report multiple test statistics to ensure robustness:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Parametric}: Cross-sectional $t$-tests for mean abnormal returns.
\item \textbf{Non-parametric}: Sign test (binomial) for the proportion of positive abnormal returns; Wilcoxon signed-rank test.
\item \textbf{Standardized}: Abnormal returns divided by cross-sectional standard deviation.
\end{itemize}

Pre-disclosure CAR[-3,$-$1] is virtually identical for positive (+0.033\%) and negative (+0.129\%) surprises, with no significant difference ($p$=0.703). CAR[0,+1] is +0.81\% for positive surprises and $-$0.47\% for negative surprises, a difference of +1.28\% ($p$<0.001). This pattern extends to CAR[+1,+5] as well ($p$=0.010). The market processes the disclosed information efficiently and in the correct direction.

\subsection{Abnormal Trading Volume}

Abnormal trading volume begins to increase from Day~$-$3 (1.05$\times$, $p$=0.004), intensifying on Day~$-$1 (1.15$\times$, $p$<0.001), and peaks on Day~+1 (1.71$\times$). The pre-disclosure volume increase suggests heightened trading activity, but given the absence of directional information in pre-disclosure returns, this likely reflects anticipatory positioning and uncertainty resolution rather than informed trading based on leaked information.

\subsection{Temporal and Cross-Sectional Heterogeneity}

We observe a structural shift in event-day CARs: positive during 2020--2022 (reflecting COVID-19 recovery earnings beats) and negative during 2024--2026 (reflecting economic headwinds). A significant quarterly pattern also emerges, with Q1 disclosures generating negative CARs ($-$0.51\%, $p$=0.010) and Q2 generating positive CARs (+0.60\%, $p$=0.003).

Cross-sectional OLS regression of CAR[-3,$-$1] on log(size) and market beta yields $R^2=0.002$, confirming that firm characteristics explain virtually none of the pre-disclosure drift---consistent with the drift being non-systematic noise.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Resolving the Paradox: Drift Without Leakage}

Our results resolve an apparent paradox. Significant pre-disclosure abnormal returns exist under parametric tests, which might suggest information leakage. However, three complementary tests demonstrate otherwise:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Direction test}: Pre-disclosure drift does not predict surprise direction (49.0\% accuracy).
\item \textbf{Non-parametric tests}: Sign test and Wilcoxon fail to confirm systematic directional drift.
\item \textbf{Surprise conditioning}: Pre-disclosure CARs are statistically identical for positive and negative surprises ($p$=0.703).
\end{enumerate}

The pre-disclosure drift observed in parametric tests appears driven by a small number of events with large positive abnormal returns, possibly reflecting: (1) company-specific news unrelated to earnings (e.g., M\&A rumors, analyst upgrades), (2) general market momentum affecting the cross-section asymmetrically, or (3) anticipatory trading based on publicly available signals that does not carry directional information about the specific earnings outcome.

\subsection{Evidence That Fair Disclosure Works}

The most compelling evidence for Fair Disclosure effectiveness comes from the surprise-direction analysis. Event-day CARs respond sharply and correctly to the earnings news: a +1.28 percentage point spread between positive and negative surprises ($p$<0.001). This clean separation indicates that the market does not know the sign of the earnings news before the official disclosure. If information were leaking, we would expect this separation to manifest in the pre-disclosure period, which it does not.

\subsection{Filing Time Implications}

Our finding that 96.5\% of filings occur after market close has methodological implications for all event studies using DART data. For after-hours filings, Day~0 is effectively a pre-disclosure day, and Day~+1 is the first true post-disclosure trading session. Future studies should account for this when defining event windows.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, our earnings surprise measure relies on text-parsed YoY operating profit changes, which may contain measurement error; only 584 of 2,208 events (26.4\%) have parseable surprise data, potentially introducing selection bias. Second, our sample is limited to large KOSPI-listed chaebol affiliates, which may not represent the broader Korean market. Third, the market model, while standard, does not control for size and value factors; reliable Korean Fama-French factor data are not readily available for our sample period. Fourth, the filing time classification relies on receipt number prefixes rather than exact timestamps. Fifth, we cannot rule out that pre-disclosure volume increases reflect legal forms of informed trading (mosaic information assembly) that do not involve Fair Disclosure violations.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This study provides evidence that South Korea's Fair Disclosure regulation effectively prevents directional information leakage in earnings announcements. While statistically significant pre-disclosure abnormal returns exist under parametric tests, three complementary analyses---direction prediction, non-parametric tests, and surprise conditioning---demonstrate that this drift is non-directional noise rather than evidence of selective information leakage.

The market's response to the actual earnings news is sharp, correctly signed, and concentrated around the disclosure date, consistent with efficient information incorporation following public release. These findings suggest that, more than two decades after its implementation, Korea's Fair Disclosure regime continues to serve its intended purpose of ensuring equitable access to material corporate information.

Our results carry a broader methodological lesson: the existence of pre-announcement drift should not be conflated with evidence of information leakage. Without conditioning on the direction of the actual news, event study evidence of pre-disclosure price movement is insufficient to draw conclusions about regulatory effectiveness.
